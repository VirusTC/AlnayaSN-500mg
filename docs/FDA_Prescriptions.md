The clinical pharmacology and mechanisms of action for prescription Nicotinic Acid under official Food and Drug Administration (FDA) labels are strictly divided into two categories: nutritional deficiency (micronutrient doses) and lipid disease management (pharmacological megadoses). [1, 2, 3] 

The following mechanisms and indications are fully sourced from the federal drug registries of the FDA Drugs Database and the [National Institutes of Health (NIH) DailyMed Repository](https://dailymed.nlm.nih.gov/). [4, 5] 
------------------------------
## I. FDA-Approved Mechanisms for Nutritional Deficiency
At low doses (14 mg to 20 mg daily), nicotinic acid functions exclusively as an essential, water-soluble B-complex vitamin. [3] 

* Clinical Indication: Pellagra (prevention and treatment). Pellagra is a severe deficiency disease clinically characterized by the "4 Ds": Dermatitis, Diarrhea, Dementia, and Death. [2, 3] 
* The Coenzyme Bio-Mechanism: Once absorbed, nicotinic acid undergoes enzymatic conversion into its active coenzyme forms: Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide (NAD+) and Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide Phosphate (NADP). [2, 6] 
* Tissue Pathology Prevention: These molecules serve as vital electron transfer carriers in dozens of metabolic pathways. They drive tissue respiration, glycolysis, and lipid metabolism. Deprivation of these coenzymes leads to widespread cellular degradation, primarily damaging high-turnover tissues like the skin, gastrointestinal tract, and central nervous system.

------------------------------
## II. FDA-Approved Mechanisms for Lipid Diseases (Dyslipidemia)
At high doses (1,000 mg to 3,000 mg daily), nicotinic acid operates via independent cellular receptor and enzyme-inhibiting drug pathways to modify lipid biomarkers. [4, 5]

According to clinical pharmacology profiles maintained by the [NIH National Library of Medicine](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK526107/), the FDA-approved lipid indications map to four distinct homeostatic mechanisms: [7] 
## 1. Secondary Myocardial Infarction Risk Reduction [8] 

* Clinical Indication: Indicated to lower the risk of recurrent, nonfatal myocardial infarctions (heart attacks) in patients with a history of hyperlipidemia and previous coronary events. [2, 4] 
* The Mechanism: Broad-spectrum modulation of circulating atherogenic particles, primarily via a significant reduction in total serum cholesterol and Apolipoprotein B-100 (Apo B). This directly cuts down the availability of circulating structural components that infiltrate the vascular endothelium to build unstable arterial plaques. [4, 5, 7] 

## 2. Severe Hypertriglyceridemia (Types IV and V Hyperlipidemia) [9, 10, 11, 12] 

* Clinical Indication: Adjunctive therapy to diet for adult patients presenting with extreme serum triglyceride levels (typically >1,000 mg/dL to 2,000 mg/dL) who carry a high risk of developing acute pancreatitis. [13, 14] 
* The Mechanism:
* Adipose Tissue Lipolysis Inhibition: Nicotinic acid binds directly to GPR109A receptors on adipocytes, decreasing intracellular cAMP and turning off Hormone-Sensitive Lipase (HSL). This stops the conversion of stored fats into free fatty acids (FFAs).
   * Hepatic DGAT2 Inhibition: Deprived of peripheral FFA influx, the liver's internal Diacylglycerol Acyltransferase 2 (DGAT2) enzyme is inhibited, halting internal triglyceride creation.
   * LPL Activation: It simultaneously increases Lipoprotein Lipase (LPL) enzyme activity in the blood vessels, accelerating the breakdown and clearing of large, bulky chylomicron triglycerides from plasma circulation. [5, 7, 15, 16, 17] 

## 3. Primary Hyperlipidemia and Mixed Dyslipidemia (Types IIa and IIb) [18] 

* Clinical Indication: Standalone or combination therapy to reduce pathologically elevated Total Cholesterol (TC), LDL-C, Apo B, and Triglycerides, and to substantially raise HDL-C levels. [4] 
* The Mechanism:
* VLDL Suppression Cascade: Because liver triglyceride synthesis is blocked, the structural protein backbone Apolipoprotein B-100 cannot be properly packaged with lipids. Unpackaged ApoB undergoes intracellular destruction, directly suppressing the liver's export of VLDL and its metabolic byproduct, LDL.
   * ApoA-I Clearance Inhibition: To raise HDL-C, nicotinic acid blocks the liver from binding, removing, and destroying Apolipoprotein A-I (ApoA-I). This extends the lifespan of active HDL particles in the blood, maximizing reverse cholesterol transport away from peripheral vessels. [5, 7, 19, 20, 21] 

## 4. Atherosclerotic Coronary Artery Disease Regression [1] 

* Clinical Indication: Prescribed as an adjunct alongside bile acid-binding resins (such as colestipol) to arrest the progression or actively promote the physical regression of plaque in patients with documented coronary artery disease (CAD). [4] 
* The Mechanism: Synergistic clearance of systemic lipids. While the resin blocks intestinal bile acid absorption (forcing the liver to burn cholesterol stores to build new bile), nicotinic acid stops the liver from pushing out new VLDL and lowers Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)] levels. This combined metabolic vacuum clears cholesterol out of vascular tissues. [4, 5, 7, 22, 23]
